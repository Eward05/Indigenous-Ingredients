The History Of Indigenous Ingredients

This blog is dedicated to showcasing the culture, heritage and traditions of the Indigenous ingredients often taken for granted by most Canadians.

Indigenous Cuisine

Indigenous Cuisine is cooking that is rich with tradition and history. It not only tells the stories of the past using local, seasonal ingredients inspired by the lands and oceans but it also emphasizes the fact that people can live in balance with their surroundings and respect all forms of life. More specifically, Indigenous ingredients and cooking draws a greater awareness to no-waste, head-to-tail cooking, something that most Canadians aren't familiar with and therefore provides them with a new perspective of our country's origins.

 

Indigenous Food Culture

In most culures around the world, food traditions are passed down from generation to generation, but the Indigenous people of Canada were stripped of that opportunity with white-washing via government-funded and church-run residential schools, ultimately erasing their cultural traditions and ceremonies. However, in recent years, there has been an increasing focus in the role that Indigenous food systems play in providing food security, while enhancing biodiversity across the world.

The Foods We Eat Today and It's Significance to Indigenous People

Wild Rice: As a staple food source for the Ojibwa people and other Nations, wild rice served as an essential source of nutrients because it is rich in flavor and high in nutrition. Not only is wild rice a native cereal but it is also the only native grain to Canada.

 

Animals: Hunting animals such as deer, elk, moose, caribou, antelope, seal, whale, walrus and polar bear all offer a wonderful opportunity to teach young children and youth about living on the land, life skills, saftey and responsibility. It not only fosters a sense of self, pride and respect for our natural enviornment, promotes culture and tradition but the meat is also higher in dietary cholesterol and less calories than domestic meats making it a healthier choice. In addition every part of the animal was consumed or used to make clothing or shelter.

 

Blueberries: Blueberrys are native to North America and Canada, growing throughout the woods and mountain regions. Indigenous people had many uses for blueberries beyond being an excellent food source. They were used for their medical properties and the act of blueberry picking provided an opportunity for socialization, story-telling and teaching.

 

Fiddleheads: Fiddlehead Greens are the premium wild forage vegetable of Spring providing a good source of vitamins A and C. Many Indigenous families on the East coast would make an outing of fiddlehead picking, while also teaching there young children how to recognize them.

 

Interesting Facts

  • Some original native recipes include roast polar bear, boiled reindeer, sweer pickled beaver, squirrel fricassee, fried woodchuck, stuffed whale breast, steamed muskrat, boiled caribou hoofs and baked skunk.
  • Early Scottish settlers first introduced bannock, a type of bread, to Canada's Indigenous people.
  • There are over 1.4 million people of Indigenous ancestry in Canada, refering to three distinct cultural groups: First Nations, Metis, and Inuit.
  • At least half of the food consumed by Indigneous people came from plants
  • The earliest utensils, including bowls, knives, spoons, grinders, and griddles, were made from all kinds of materials, such as rock and animal bones.

 

Indigenous-owned Eateries Found In Canada

    ↪ Salmon n' Bannock Bistro Located in Vancouver. B.C

The bistro known as Salmon n' Bannock, owned by Inez and Remi remains to be Vancouver's only First Nations restaurant, specializing in wild fish, free range game meat and bannock which is all baked fresh daily. 

 

    ↪ Little Chief Hotel Restaurant Located in Calgary, AB

Situated inside the Grey Eagle Hotel and Casino on Calgary's Tsuu T'ina Nations, the "land to table" aspect of First Nations living is honoured by the principle that everything served is sourced and foraged directly from local Indigenous lands and their communities. 

 

    ↪ Pow Wow Cafe Located in Toronto, ON

Shawn Adler and his team have been serving creative tacos on Ojibway-style frybread for almost a decade in the Kensington Market neighbourhood of Toronto. 

 

Recipes On The Wild Side

Sautéed Fiddlehead Fern With Parsley And Garlic

  • 1 - Pound fiddleheads
  • 1 or 2 Garlic cloves, minced very fine
  • 1/4 cup Butter or extra-virgin olive oil
  • 2 tbsp Finely chopped fresh parsley 
  • Salt and fresh ground black pepper

Trim and wash fiddle heads in cold water, removing the brown covering as you go. Drain and pat dry. Heat half of the butter (or olive oil) in a large skillet over medium heat. Add the fern shoots and turn the heat up to medium-high. The ferns should sizzle, but don't allow the butter to burn. Toss and stir for about 5 minutes. Add the other half of the butter (or oil), the garlic, and the parsley. Continue cooking for one minute longer, or until you can smell the garlic and the ferns are tender. Season with salt and pepper and serve immediately on hot plates! 

 

Moose Roast or Elk Roast Baked In Foil

  • 3-4 lb. Roast
  • 1/2 pkg. of dry onion soup 

Preheat oven to 450 degrees. Place roast on a piece of heavy duty aluminum foil. Sprinkle 1/2 pkg of dry onion soup over meat. Bring edges of foil together and seal tightly. Place in shallow roasting pan and bake for 2 to 2 1/2 hours. There will be ample juice collected inside the foil which can be thickened for gravy! 

 

Bannock Recipe II

  • 3 c Flour
  • 1/2 tsp Salt
  • Vegetable oil for frying
  • 1 1/2 tsp Baking powder
  • 1 1/3 c Warm water

Mix the flour, baking powder and salt together. Add water and knead the dough until soft. Roll the dough until 1/4 inches thick, then cut out rounds 4 inches in diameter. Fry the bread in 2 or 3 inches of hot oil until puffed and browned on both sides. Serve and enjoy!
